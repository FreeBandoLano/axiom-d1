1. PROBLEM DEFINITION AND CONTEXT (30 marks)
------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM STATEMENT

    Urban drainage infrastructure across the Caribbean, particularly in
Trinidad and Tobago, relies on networks of open concrete channels ranging
from 0.45 m to 1.2 m in width and 0.3 m to 0.6 m in depth [1]. These
drains accumulate solid waste — plastic bottles, bags, organic matter — and
develop thick algae biofilm on their walls. Blockages restrict hydraulic
capacity, causing severe urban flooding during the wet season (June –
December) that damages property and displaces residents [2]. Standing water
in clogged drains also creates breeding grounds for Aedes aegypti
mosquitoes, the primary vector for dengue, Zika, and chikungunya — diseases
that impose significant public health burdens on Caribbean Small Island
Developing States (SIDS) [3].

    Current maintenance is almost entirely manual: municipal workers enter
drains with shovels and rakes to remove debris. This approach exposes
workers to leptospirosis (contracted through contact with contaminated
water), chemical hazards, sharp refuse, and physical injury [4]. Manual
cleaning is also labour-intensive, infrequent, and reactive — drains are
typically cleaned only after blockages cause flooding rather than through
preventive maintenance. A robotics-based solution is appropriate because it
removes human workers from hazardous drain environments, enables more
frequent and systematic cleaning schedules, and can operate in the shallow
water conditions that make drains inaccessible to conventional heavy
equipment.

2. CONCEPTUAL ROBOTICS APPROACH (40 marks)
------------------------------------------------------------------------

ROBOT/PLATFORM SELECTION AND JUSTIFICATION

    [SYSTEM NAME] is a compact, tracked amphibious robot designed
specifically for shallow-drain maintenance. A tracked (caterpillar)
platform was selected over wheeled or legged alternatives for the following
engineering reasons:

    (a) Traction — Rubber tracks provide continuous ground contact across
        wet, silt-covered, and algae-slippery drain floors where wheels
        would lose grip [5].
    (b) Obstacle traversal — Tracks can climb over small debris
        accumulations and raised drain joints (up to ~5 cm) without
        becoming stuck.
    (c) Load distribution — The wider contact patch prevents the robot from
        sinking into soft sediment layers that accumulate in unmaintained
        drains.
    (d) Amphibious operation — Rubber tracks function in both dry and
        shallow-water conditions without modification, unlike wheel-based
        systems that require separate waterproofing for hub bearings.
    (e) Differential steering — Two independently driven tracks enable
        zero-radius turning, critical for manoeuvring within the confined
        width of drainage channels.

    The platform integrates three functional mechanisms:
    • Front-mounted scoop and conveyor belt for solid waste collection
    • Side-mounted rotating nylon brush assemblies for algae scrubbing
    • Onboard sealed collection bin (~5 L) with mesh drainage

    This combination addresses both primary drain maintenance tasks — debris
removal and biofilm cleaning — in a single pass, inspired by amphibious
garbage-collection robot designs in the literature [6].

    1. Locomotion — Two 12 V DC gear motors (each ~3 A under load) drive
       rubber caterpillar tracks via sprocket-and-chain. Differential speed
       control enables steering.
    2. Collection — A front-mounted scoop (servo-actuated tilt) feeds debris
       onto a short conveyor belt (small DC motor) that deposits waste into a
       sealed 5 L collection bin with mesh drain holes.
    3. Cleaning — Two rotating nylon brush assemblies (12 V DC, ~1 A each) on
       adjustable mounts scrub algae from drain floor and walls.
    4. Sensing — Two ultrasonic sensors (HC-SR04) for forward and rear
       obstacle detection; MPU6050 IMU for tilt/orientation; resistive water-
       level sensor to monitor depth.
    5. Control — ESP32-CAM serves as main controller, handling WiFi
       communication, live video streaming, and GPIO control of motor
       drivers.
    6. Communication — 2.4 GHz WiFi link to a mobile app providing live
       camera feed and directional controls; range ~50 m line-of-sight.
    7. Power — 12 V, 10 Ah LiPo battery pack with battery management system
       (BMS) and 15 A fuse protection; total capacity 120 Wh.

3. SUSTAINABILITY AND SDG ALIGNMENT (20 marks)
------------------------------------------------------------------------

SDG RELEVANCE

    [SYSTEM NAME] directly addresses United Nations Sustainable Development
Goal 6: Clean Water and Sanitation, specifically Target 6.3: "By 2030,
improve water quality by reducing pollution, eliminating dumping and
minimizing release of hazardous chemicals and materials, halving the
proportion of untreated wastewater and substantially increasing recycling
and safe reuse globally" [7].

    Urban drains serve as the primary conduit through which land-based solid
waste enters rivers, wetlands, and coastal waters in Trinidad and Tobago.
By systematically removing debris and algae from drainage channels before
they reach downstream water bodies, the robot directly reduces pollutant
loading on aquatic ecosystems. Additionally, the system supports SDG Target
11.5 (reduce deaths and losses from water-related disasters) by restoring
hydraulic capacity to blocked drains, thereby mitigating urban flood risk.

ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL IMPACT

    Environmental benefits:
    • Each deployment session can remove an estimated 2–5 kg of solid waste
      from drainage channels, diverting this material from waterways.
    • Regular algae removal prevents eutrophication-driven oxygen depletion
      in downstream water bodies.
    • Battery-powered operation produces zero direct emissions during use.
    • At a municipal scale, weekly robotic cleaning of priority drain
      sections (~5 km) could intercept an estimated 50–130 kg of waste per
      month before it reaches the marine environment.

    Social and community benefits:
    • Eliminates direct human contact with contaminated drain water,
      reducing occupational exposure to leptospirosis and waterborne
      pathogens.
    • Proactive drain maintenance reduces urban flooding frequency,
      protecting vulnerable low-income communities disproportionately
      affected by flood damage [2].
    • Low prototype cost (~$360) and use of widely available components
      make the system replicable and affordable for Caribbean municipal
      authorities.
    • Requires minimal operator training (mobile-app-based control with
      live video), enabling deployment by existing municipal maintenance
      teams.

4. SUMMARY
------------------------------------------------------------------------

    [SYSTEM NAME] is a compact, tracked amphibious robot that autonomously
navigates shallow open drains to collect solid waste via a front-mounted
scoop-and-conveyor system and scrub algae using rotating brush assemblies.
By addressing SDG 6 (Clean Water and Sanitation), the system reduces
pollutant discharge into Caribbean waterways, mitigates urban flood risk
from blocked drains, and eliminates hazardous manual labour — offering a
low-cost, replicable solution for proactive urban drain maintenance in
Small Island Developing States.
